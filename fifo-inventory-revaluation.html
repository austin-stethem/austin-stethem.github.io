1. Situation & Task: The Inflationary Lag

The Enterprise Scenario (Step 5 of 5) Our statistical A/B tracking (Step 4) confirms our new price points are holding firm in the market without sacrificing volume. Now we arrive at the most lucrative part of the cycle. Because our supply chain teams secured 60 days of inventory before the 15% cost spike hit, and we decoupled our retail price from our physical receiving docks, we are selling cheap stock at the newly elevated retail tiers. We must model this immediate EBITDA windfall.

The Context: In physical distribution, when a manufacturer announces a sudden 10% price increase, the instinct of a reactive pricing team is to wait. They often hold prices steady until the new, expensive inventory physically arrives on the shelves, believing this is the "fair" way to treat customers.

The Task: This reactive mindset destroys potential capital. In reality, a mature distribution network often holds 60 to 90 days of Quantity On Hand (QOH) that was purchased at the old, cheaper cost. I was tasked with engineering a financial strategy to decouple our retail price logic from our physical receiving docks, transforming supply chain inflation into an immediate P&L advantage.

2. Action: Pricing on Replacement Cost

I transitioned the organizational pricing mechanism away from Average Cost and strictly onto Replacement Cost. By architecting this business logic into the ERP, the system instantly triggers a retail markup the moment a vendor price increase is announced—regardless of what is sitting on our shelves.

Systems Integration (The Ecosystem): In reality, vendor cost increases are never a flat percentage across all SKUs. As demonstrated in my Algorithmic Margin Defense and Stochastic Optimization models, cost shocks vary wildly by item. Those engines handle the micro-level elasticity—protecting KVIs and finding margin on elastic items—to ultimately calculate a Weighted Average Cost Spike for the portfolio. It is that finalized, blended deficit that feeds into this macro-level FIFO model.

Because accounting strictly follows FIFO (First-In, First-Out), we are now selling inventory that was bought cheaply, but charging the customer the new, inflated market rate. This gap between the old physical cost and the new retail price creates a temporary, massive spike in margin: The EBITDA Windfall.
