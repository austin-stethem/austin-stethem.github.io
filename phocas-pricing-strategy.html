1. Executive Project Summary & Business Context

The Enterprise Scenario (Step 4 of 5) The algorithmic guardrails (Step 3) are actively defending our margins in the ERP against the 15% vendor cost spike. However, the architecture is only as strong as its feedback loop. Before we calculate our final cash windfall, we must monitor our Key Value Items (KVIs) in real-time using Phocas BI to ensure the new pricing hasn't triggered volume destruction in our highly contested territories. We shift from deployment to continuous BI surveillance.

Historically, pricing adjustments at the enterprise level were reactive, driven largely by field-level complaints or sporadic customer feedback—a "loudest voice" heuristic that inherently lagged behind market volatility across our 80-branch network.

To eliminate this administrative drag, I architected and manage a Dynamic Weighted Indexing engine that shifts our pricing operations from passive spreadsheet management to proactive, forecast-driven margin defense. By synthesizing usage volume, competitor pricing, and seasonal logistics cost-vectors, this system identifies Known Value Items (KVIs) and automates the pricing logic for entire product families.

2. The Logic of KVI Identification & Data Synthesis

Defining a Known Value Item (KVI) requires more than just analyzing sales volume; it requires a cross-functional consensus on what constitutes a "price-sensitive" anchor. The KVI identification engine is a collaborative output between the Product Analytics team (inventory procurement), Information Software leads (database/ERP infrastructure), and the Pricing department.

We filter SKUs through a multi-variable funnel that includes 12-month rolling usage data, margin thresholds (factoring in freight, surcharges, and warranty cost-delta), and market sentiment-curated recommendations from branch managers and field sales agents who possess ground-level visibility on price friction.

3. Price Waterfall Mechanics & Competitive Meta-Modeling

Rather than performing manual, line-by-line price changes, I utilize a "Meta-Modeling" approach to pricing. Once we establish a definitive "meta" price point for a core product range (e.g., a specific line of brake components), the system automatically flows pricing adjustments to likeminded numbers within that product group.

When pricing a specific SKU, the model automatically executes a "Price Waterfall," calculating variables including sales velocity, logistics overhead (freight charges and surcharges), and localized competitive gaps. This allows the pricing engine to scale exponentially; a single logic update ripples across the catalog, zeroing in on competitive gaps based on current market research.
