1. Executive Project Summary & Business Context

Enterprise pricing models often rely on deterministic forecasting—calculating next year's margins based on last year's averages. However, in a volatile supply chain environment, averages mask risk. To shift from descriptive analytics to predictive risk management, I engineered a Python-based Monte Carlo simulation. This programmatic model stress-tests proposed pricing strategies against thousands of randomized market volatility scenarios, providing executive leadership with probability distributions rather than static guesses.

2. Injecting Chaos: Programmatic Uncertainty Optimization

Instead of assuming flat percentage adjustments, the Python script samples distributions from historical data streams via NumPy to evaluate compounding volatility concurrently across 10,000 algorithmic permutations:

import numpy as np

def run_compound_risk_simulation(base_cost, freight_mean, freight_std, iterations=10000):
    freight_shocks = np.random.normal(freight_mean, freight_std, iterations)
    competitor_erosion = np.random.uniform(0.02, 0.08, iterations)
    simulated_landed_costs = base_cost * (1 + competitor_erosion) + freight_shocks
    worst_case_tail = np.percentile(simulated_landed_costs, 95)
    return {"p95_risk_boundary": worst_case_tail}

3. From Probability to Executive Action

A data science model is only valuable if it drives a business decision. The output of this Python simulation translates raw operational variance into structured risk corridors, forcing explicit preventative pricing guardrails before matrix release updates are pushed down to checkout networks. By actively setting our price floors at the P95 margin instead of the P50 median, we structurally prevent margin compression during supply chain shocks.
