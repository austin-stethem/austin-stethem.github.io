The Enterprise Scenario (Step 1 of 5) Before we can execute defensive pricing strategies across the distribution network, we must first understand our exact geographic exposure. Relying on static delivery radii leaves operations blind to regional competitor density and market cannibalization. Our first objective is to build a spatial intelligence engine that maps these threats in real-time, pinpointing exactly where our market leverage is strong and where our territories are highly vulnerable.

1. The Problem: From Geometric Guesswork to Predictive Capture

In high-velocity distribution, defining sales territories with a static "20-mile radius" is a recipe for margin erosion. A 20-mile delivery in gridlocked downtown Atlanta destroys logistics profitability, while a 20-mile route in rural Iowa represents a highly profitable, captive market. To move from descriptive reporting to prescriptive revenue capture, I replaced this static guesswork with a dynamic Geospatial Intelligence Engine.

By programmatically harvesting and mapping thousands of competitor locations against our internal Hub & Spoke network, the engine exposed our exact territorial blind spots. This allowed operations leadership to instantly visualize market friction, identifying precisely where our routes were exposed to competitor cannibalization, and where we had the geographic leverage to safely raise prices.

2. Machine Learning: Translating Maps into Margin

A map is just a picture until you attach revenue to it. To translate this geography into an actionable pricing strategy, I engineered a POS Gravity Engine. By extracting 12 months of ERP invoice data and geocoding thousands of Customer Ship-To addresses, the Python engine calculated the exact mathematical "Distance Decay" of our revenue—proving exactly how far customers were willing to travel, or wait for delivery, before defecting to a competitor.

I then fed these decay curves into an unsupervised K-Means Machine Learning Algorithm (via scikit-learn). Instead of relying on subjective opinions from regional managers, the algorithm mathematically clustered our 80-branch network into 5 distinct Strategic Topology Tiers based on their actual friction profiles.

This fundamentally changed how the enterprise priced its catalog. We now knew with mathematical certainty which branches operated as "Tier 1: Rural Monopolies" (capable of absorbing aggressive, network-funding price increases) and which were "Tier 5: Hyper-Dense Urban" (requiring strict, defensive margin floors to survive block-by-block warfare without destroying volume).

3. Enterprise Ecosystem: Scaling the Architecture

This geospatial engine was not a single-use script; it was architected as a foundational data ecosystem. Using this exact Python & SQL backbone, I expanded the architecture to solve massive operational inefficiencies across the enterprise:

  • M&A Poaching & Cannibalization: Modeled regional overlaps between merging competitors to identify "cross-street" store closures, allowing our outside sales reps to preemptively target and poach abandoned commercial accounts.
  • Greenfield Expansion (Whitespace): Identified high-density VIO corridors completely void of our distribution hubs, generating data-backed expansion targets for executive leadership.
  • Logistics & Fleet Optimization: Modified the engine to map physical delivery vectors, identifying severe overlapping territories to eliminate margin waste on fuel, driver overtime, and fleet wear-and-tear.
  • Sales Territory Calibration: Visualized actual POS revenue vectors against assigned salesman routes to expose under-serviced regions and rebalance rep workloads.

Cross-Functional Enterprise Adoption

While initially architected to solve regional pricing elasticity, the underlying spatial engine was designed to be highly modular. Following an executive demo, the foundation was rapidly scaled across multiple departments. The core architecture was adapted to generate logistics spider-maps for freight and route optimization, competitor density models for Sales & Marketing, and Greenfield/M&A threat-monitoring dashboards utilized directly by the Company President. The objective was not to build a single-use script, but a flexible, scalable enterprise tool.
