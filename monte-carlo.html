1. The Problem: Deterministic Forecasting in a Volatile World

Enterprise pricing models often rely on deterministic forecasting—calculating next year's corporate margins based on last year's static averages. However, in a volatile supply chain, averages mask catastrophic risk. If a logistics disruption coincides with aggressive competitor pricing, a standard spreadsheet model will fail to predict the compounding margin erosion.

To shift the Pricing Department from descriptive reporting to predictive risk management, I engineered a Python-based Monte Carlo simulation. This model utilizes vectorized operations to stress-test proposed pricing strategies against tens of thousands of randomized macroeconomic scenarios in seconds, providing executive leadership with probability distributions rather than static guesses.

2. Vectorized Probability Mechanics (The Python Engine)

The core power of a Monte Carlo simulation lies in programmatic uncertainty. Instead of assuming flat percentage adjustments, the engine samples continuous variables across normal and uniform distributions. By utilizing NumPy arrays, the system processes these independent macroeconomic variables concurrently across 50,000 distinct permutations.

$$\text{Expected Margin} = \frac{\text{Sale Price} - (\text{Base Cost} \cdot (1 + \text{Comp Erosion}) + \text{Freight Volatility})}{\text{Sale Price}}$$ 
import numpy as np

def run_compound_risk_simulation(base_cost, freight_mean, freight_std, iterations=50000):
    # Vectorized array execution for high-velocity distribution modeling
    freight_shocks = np.random.normal(freight_mean, freight_std, iterations)
    competitor_erosion = np.random.uniform(0.01, 0.08, iterations)
    
    # Simulating 50,000 distinct landed-cost permutations instantly
    simulated_landed_costs = base_cost * (1 + competitor_erosion) + freight_shocks
    
    # Extracting the P95 tail (Worst-case boundary)
    p95_risk_boundary = np.percentile(simulated_landed_costs, 95)
    return p95_risk_boundary
